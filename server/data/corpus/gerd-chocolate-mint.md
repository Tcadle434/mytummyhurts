# Chocolate and Mint — Lower Esophageal Sphincter Relaxation

Chocolate and peppermint share a mechanism in reflux: both are associated with relaxing the lower esophageal sphincter (LES), the valve that keeps stomach acid down. This document covers the chocolate/mint family for gastroesophageal reflux (GERD). Individual tolerance still varies.

## Chocolate and the LES

Chocolate and cocoa are commonly reported reflux triggers, and the usual explanation is relaxation of the lower esophageal sphincter, which lets stomach acid rise into the esophagus more easily. Cocoa contains methylxanthines (including a small amount of caffeine and theobromine) that are thought to contribute to this loosening of the valve; chocolate is also often high in fat, which independently slows gastric emptying and can add to the effect. Dietary guidance for GERD from NIDDK and MedlinePlus frequently lists chocolate among foods people may find worsen heartburn. Because richer, higher-cocoa and higher-fat chocolate carries more of these compounds per serving, larger or richer portions tend to be reported as stronger triggers, though the response differs between individuals.

## Peppermint and Spearmint

Peppermint and spearmint are traditional reflux triggers because mint can relax the lower esophageal sphincter, reducing the barrier that holds acid in the stomach. This is a notable point because mint is often assumed to "soothe" digestion — peppermint may ease some digestive complaints, yet in reflux the same sphincter-relaxing effect can make heartburn worse. Common sources include peppermint tea, after-dinner mints, mint candies, and mint-flavored products taken at the end of a meal, exactly when reflux risk is already elevated. Guidance for GERD commonly suggests watching mint for this reason. As with chocolate, the effect is not universal, so mint is worth testing as a personal trigger rather than treated as forbidden.
