# Lactose-Free Milk, Cultured Dairy, and Why Aged Cheese Is Tolerated

Not every dairy food challenges someone with lactose intolerance to the same degree. The symptoms come from undigested lactose fermenting in the colon (NIDDK), so any process that removes lactose, pre-digests it, or supplies the missing enzyme tends to make a dairy food easier to tolerate. Three approaches account for most of the "lower-trouble" dairy on the shelf: lactose-free milk where the sugar is already split, live-culture yogurt where bacteria have consumed some lactose, and aged cheese where both whey removal and ripening have stripped lactose out. Tolerance is individual and dose-related, but these mechanisms explain the pattern.

## Lactose-Free Milk Is Pre-Split, Not Dairy-Free

Lactose-free milk is ordinary cow's milk with the enzyme lactase added, which breaks the lactose down into its two component simple sugars (glucose and galactose) before the milk is ever consumed (NIDDK, MedlinePlus). It is still real dairy — it contains the same protein and fat as regular milk — but the sugar that causes fermentation symptoms has already been split, so it is generally well tolerated by people with lactose intolerance. Because glucose and galactose are sweeter than lactose, lactose-free milk often tastes slightly sweeter than regular milk even with nothing added. Lactase drops and tablets work on the same principle, supplying the enzyme so lactose can be digested.

## Live-Culture Yogurt Is Often Better Tolerated

Yogurt made with live active bacterial cultures is frequently tolerated better than an equivalent amount of milk (NIDDK). Two things help. First, the bacteria used to ferment yogurt consume some of the milk's lactose as they turn it into lactic acid, so the finished yogurt contains less lactose than the milk it started from. Second, the live cultures themselves carry lactase enzyme that can continue to help break down lactose in the gut. Thicker strained yogurts have had watery whey (and the lactose in it) drained off, lowering the lactose further. Yogurt is still a source of lactose, so tolerance varies, but the mechanism explains why it tends to sit easier than plain milk.

## Why Aged Cheese Passes When Milk Does Not

Aged cheeses such as cheddar, parmesan, gouda, and Swiss are low enough in lactose that many people who react to milk tolerate them (NIDDK, MedlinePlus). The lactose is removed in two stages. When curds are separated from the liquid whey, most of the lactose drains away with the whey. Then, during ripening, the cheese's bacterial cultures ferment much of the small amount that remains, and the longer a cheese ages the more is consumed. A hard, well-aged cheese can therefore contain only trace lactose. This is why cheese cannot be judged as a single category: an aged cheese and a fresh, unripened cheese behave very differently for lactose.

## Tolerance Is Individual and Dose-Related

How much dairy a person can handle varies widely, and the same food can be fine in a small amount but troublesome in a large one, because lactose symptoms scale with the dose that reaches the colon (NIDDK). Many people with lactose intolerance can manage modest amounts of lactose, especially when it is spread across the day or eaten with other foods rather than on an empty stomach. Choosing lower-lactose forms — lactose-free milk, live-culture or strained yogurt, aged hard cheese, butter — lets someone keep dairy in the diet while keeping the lactose load down. These are descriptions of how the foods behave, not a treatment plan.
