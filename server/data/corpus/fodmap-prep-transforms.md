# Preparation and FODMAPs — What Changes the Load and What Does Not

How a food is prepared can raise or lower its FODMAP content, but the effect follows specific rules from the Monash University framework. Some steps genuinely remove FODMAPs; others that people assume will help do nothing. Knowing which is which prevents both needless avoidance and false confidence.

## Water-Soluble, Not Oil-Soluble

The FODMAPs that dominate savoury cooking — fructans and GOS — are water-soluble and do not dissolve into fat or oil. This property underlies the most useful preparation trick in the framework: infusing oil with garlic or onion captures their oil-soluble flavour compounds while leaving the water-soluble fructans behind in the discarded solids, so Monash University rates garlic-infused oil (solids removed) as low-FODMAP even though garlic itself is high. The same water-solubility means that steeping or simmering a high-fructan aromatic in a water-based liquid does the opposite: the fructans dissolve into the broth or sauce and stay in the dish. The rule of thumb is that fat carries flavour without fructans, while water carries the fructans along with it.

## Boiling and Leaching into Water

Because fructans and GOS dissolve in water, cooking a food in plenty of water and then discarding that water can carry some FODMAPs away. This is the mechanism behind draining and rinsing canned legumes, where GOS leaches into the canning liquid that is then poured off, and it is why Monash University notes canned-and-rinsed legumes tend to be lower in FODMAPs than the same beans cooked from dried and served in their cooking liquid. The key is that the FODMAP-laden water must actually be discarded — if the cooking liquid becomes the sauce or soup base, the leached FODMAPs go straight back into the meal. This differs sharply from steaming, roasting, or frying, none of which give the FODMAPs a water phase to leach into.

## Cooking Does Not "Cook Out" FODMAPs

A common misconception is that heat destroys FODMAPs the way it can break down some other compounds. It does not. FODMAP carbohydrates are stable to ordinary cooking temperatures, so frying onion, roasting garlic, sautéing mushrooms, or baking wheat does not eliminate their fructans, mannitol, or other FODMAPs — the load is essentially still present in the finished dish. In the Monash University framework, softening or caramelising a high-FODMAP vegetable changes its flavour and texture but not its FODMAP status. This is why "well-cooked" is not a route to tolerance for garlic and onion, and why roasted cauliflower still carries mannitol. The only preparation steps that lower FODMAPs are those that physically remove them (leaching into discarded water, pressing out liquid) or reduce them biologically (fermentation).

## Ripeness and Storage Shift the Load

FODMAP content is not fixed within a food; it changes as produce ripens and ages. Monash University notes that ripening shifts a fruit's carbohydrate profile — the well-known example is the banana, where a firm, just-ripe banana is low-FODMAP but a very ripe, spotty banana accumulates more oligofructans and moves higher. Ripeness can therefore change the verdict on the same fruit from one day to the next. Storage matters too: some vegetables develop or lose FODMAPs over time. The practical point is that "how ripe" and "how old" are part of a food's FODMAP identity alongside "how much," so the same banana or piece of fruit is not a fixed quantity from purchase to overripe.

## Fermentation and Sourdough

Fermentation is the one biological process that reliably lowers certain FODMAPs, because the microbes consume them. In a long sourdough fermentation, wild yeasts and lactic-acid bacteria break down some of the fructans in the dough before baking, so a traditionally fermented loaf can be lower in fructans than conventionally leavened bread — Monash University notes traditional sourdough, especially spelt sourdough, can be lower in FODMAPs. Fermentation also underlies why firm tofu and tempeh differ from raw soy. The important caveat is that fermentation must be genuine and sufficiently long to reduce the FODMAP; a short process or added souring flavour will not do it. Fermentation is thus the counterpart to leaching — a biological, rather than physical, way to remove FODMAPs.
