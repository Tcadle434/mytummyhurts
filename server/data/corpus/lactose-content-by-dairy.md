# Lactose Content Varies Enormously by Dairy Type

Lactose is the natural sugar in milk. People with lactose intolerance make less of the enzyme lactase, so undigested lactose ferments in the colon and draws in water, producing gas, bloating, and diarrhea (NIDDK). A crucial and often-missed point is that the amount of lactose differs dramatically between dairy foods — "dairy" is not one thing. Fresh liquid dairy is high in lactose, while aging and fat-separation strip most of it out. Knowing where a specific food falls explains why one person can eat aged cheese comfortably but reacts to a glass of milk.

## Milk and Ice Cream Carry the Most Lactose

Cow's milk is the reference high-lactose food: lactose is dissolved throughout the water portion of milk and none has been removed or fermented, so a standard glass delivers a substantial dose (NIDDK, MedlinePlus). Ice cream and milkshakes are milk-based and typically sweetened dairy, so they also carry a high lactose load, and large or second servings stack the dose higher. Because symptoms in lactose intolerance are dose-related, portion size matters as much as the food itself — a splash of milk in coffee behaves very differently from a full glass. Evaporated and condensed milk concentrate the milk solids and are correspondingly high in lactose.

## Soft, Fresh Cheeses Stay High

Soft fresh cheeses that are not aged — such as ricotta, cottage cheese, cream cheese, and fresh mozzarella — retain much of the milk's original lactose because they are made quickly and still hold a lot of the watery whey where lactose lives (NIDDK). Fresh cheeses generally sit toward the higher-lactose end of the cheese spectrum, closer to milk than to aged cheese. This is the key contrast with hard aged cheese: it is not the word "cheese" that predicts lactose, but how the cheese was made and how long it was aged. Cheese sauces and processed cheese products made with added milk or whey can also raise the lactose load.

## Aged Hard Cheeses Are Very Low in Lactose

Aged hard cheeses — cheddar, parmesan, Swiss, gouda, and similar — contain very little lactose and are often well tolerated even by people who react to milk (NIDDK, MedlinePlus). Two mechanisms remove the lactose. First, most lactose leaves with the liquid whey when the curds are separated and pressed. Second, the bacterial cultures used to make the cheese consume much of the remaining lactose as the cheese ripens, and longer aging means more is broken down. The result is that a well-aged cheese can approach near-zero lactose. This is why the app should not treat "cheese" as uniformly high-lactose: an aged cheddar and a fresh ricotta belong on opposite ends.

## Butter Is Nearly Lactose-Free

Butter is made from the fat (cream) fraction of milk, and lactose lives in the water portion, not the fat. Because butter is mostly milkfat with very little residual milk solids and water, it contains only trace lactose and is generally tolerated by people with lactose intolerance (NIDDK). Clarified butter and ghee have had nearly all remaining milk solids removed, lowering residual lactose even further. As with aged cheese, the general principle is that separating milk into its fractions — pulling off the fat, draining the whey — is what concentrates or removes lactose, so the finished food's lactose depends on which fraction it came from and how it was processed.
