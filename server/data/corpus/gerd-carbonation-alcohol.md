# Carbonation and Alcohol — Gastric Distension and Sphincter Effects

Carbonated drinks and alcohol are frequent reflux triggers, but they act through different mechanisms. This document covers the carbonation/alcohol family for gastroesophageal reflux (GERD). Individual tolerance varies, and the two are grouped here because both are common in the same beverages (beer, sparkling wine).

## Carbonation and Gastric Distension

Carbonated beverages — sodas, sparkling water, tonic, and other fizzy drinks — deliver carbon dioxide gas that expands the stomach. This gastric distension is the key mechanism: a stretched, gas-filled stomach promotes transient relaxations of the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) and raises pressure on that valve, both of which make reflux and belching more likely. The gas can carry stomach contents upward, and the burping that follows a fizzy drink is a moment when acid can escape into the esophagus. Guidance for GERD from bodies such as NIDDK and MedlinePlus commonly lists carbonated drinks among reflux triggers. Notably, this distension effect is largely about the carbonation itself, so even unsweetened sparkling water can provoke symptoms in sensitive people, while flat water does not.

## Alcohol: LES Relaxation Plus Acid

Alcohol contributes to reflux through two mechanisms at once. First, alcohol can relax the lower esophageal sphincter, weakening the barrier that normally keeps acid in the stomach. Second, alcohol can stimulate gastric acid secretion, increasing the acid available to reflux. This combination — a looser valve and more acid — is why alcoholic drinks are a common heartburn trigger for people with GERD. The effect tends to scale with the amount consumed and is often worse when drinking late in the evening or lying down afterward. Response is individually variable, and some drinks compound the problem: beer adds carbonation on top of alcohol, and evening drinking close to bedtime overlaps with the reflux risk of lying down soon after intake.
