# Histamine, Aged and Fermented Foods

Histamine is a natural compound found in many foods and formed by bacteria during aging, fermentation, and spoilage. Some people appear to be sensitive to dietary histamine — often described as histamine intolerance, thought to involve reduced activity of the enzyme (diamine oxidase) that breaks histamine down in the gut — and may report flushing, headache, hives, nasal congestion, or digestive upset after high-histamine foods. It is important to be honest about the evidence: histamine intolerance is a real but less-established sensitivity, and the science is weaker and more individual than for lactose intolerance or the FODMAP approach to IBS. Reactions vary a great deal between people, reliable food-histamine numbers are hard to pin down, and much of the guidance comes from elimination-diet experience rather than large trials. The sections below describe which foods *tend* to be higher in histamine and why, framed as possibilities, not certainties.

## Why Aging and Fermentation Raise Histamine

Histamine is produced when bacteria convert the amino acid histidine into histamine, so foods that involve microbial action or long storage tend to accumulate more of it. Fermentation deliberately encourages bacterial activity, and aging gives bacteria time to work, which is why fermented and aged products are commonly listed among higher-histamine foods in the food-chemical and elimination-diet literature (such as the RPAH/Allergy Unit approach). Histamine is also heat-stable, so ordinary cooking does not reliably destroy what has already formed. Freshness is therefore the recurring theme across histamine-sensitive eating: the same food is generally lower in histamine when very fresh and higher after aging, fermenting, or prolonged storage. Because individual sensitivity and actual histamine content both vary widely, these are tendencies rather than fixed rules.

## Aged Cheese May Be High; Fresh Cheese Lower

Aged and ripened cheeses — such as aged cheddar, parmesan, gouda, blue cheese, and other long-matured varieties — are frequently cited as higher-histamine foods, because the bacterial cultures and the long ripening time allow histamine to build up. Fresh, unaged dairy such as fresh mozzarella, ricotta, cottage cheese, and cream cheese tends to be lower in histamine, since little aging has occurred. This is a useful contrast with lactose: for lactose, aging removes the trouble (aged cheese is low-lactose), whereas for histamine, aging may add it. So the same aged cheese can be a good choice for someone with lactose intolerance yet a possible trigger for someone who is histamine-sensitive. Individual tolerance differs, and the histamine content of a given cheese is variable.

## Cured and Processed Meats May Be High

Cured, fermented, aged, and smoked meats — such as salami, pepperoni, prosciutto, other dry-cured hams, and many processed deli meats — are commonly grouped among higher-histamine foods because curing and aging give bacteria time to generate histamine from the meat proteins. Fish is a special case: histamine can rise quickly in fish that is not kept cold, and scombroid poisoning (an illness affecting anyone, not only histamine-sensitive people) is caused by high histamine in improperly stored fish, which is why very fresh or promptly frozen fish is generally lower. For histamine-sensitive individuals, fresh, well-chilled, promptly cooked meat and fish tend to be better tolerated than cured or aged versions. As with all histamine claims, actual content varies and reactions are individual.

## Fermented Vegetables and Condiments May Be High

Fermented foods are among the most consistently listed higher-histamine items in elimination-diet guidance, because fermentation is bacterial activity by design. Sauerkraut and kimchi (fermented cabbage), kombucha (fermented tea), soy sauce, miso, and other fermented soy products are frequently named, along with vinegar-fermented items. The mechanism is the same throughout: bacteria producing histamine over the course of fermentation, with heat-stable histamine that is not reliably removed by later cooking. Some people who are histamine-sensitive report tolerating small amounts, and others do not, which reflects how individual this sensitivity appears to be. Because the evidence for histamine intolerance is more limited than for lactose or FODMAPs, these foods are best described as *possible* triggers whose effect is worth observing per person rather than assumed.
