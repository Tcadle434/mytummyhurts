# Caffeine Across Conditions — Colonic Motility in IBS, LES Relaxation in Reflux

Caffeine is a cross-condition trigger that pulls in two directions depending on which part of the gut you are looking at. At the lower end, caffeine stimulates movement of the colon, which can worsen urgency and diarrhea in IBS. At the upper end, caffeine is associated with relaxation of the lower esophageal sphincter, which can promote reflux in GERD. Coffee, tea, and energy drinks all deliver caffeine, but coffee in particular has effects that go beyond caffeine alone — decaf still shows some reflux activity. This document keeps the IBS route and the GERD route separate, since the same cup can matter for two different reasons.

## Caffeine Stimulates Colonic Motility — the IBS Route

Caffeine is a stimulant that increases contractile activity in the colon, which is why a morning coffee is a well-known prompt for a bowel movement in many people. For someone with IBS — especially the diarrhea-predominant pattern — that extra push on colonic motility can translate into urgency, cramping, and looser or more frequent stools, so caffeinated drinks are commonly listed among IBS dietary triggers (NIDDK, MedlinePlus). The effect is dose-related in a general sense: a large or strong coffee tends to stimulate more than a small weak one, and sensitivity varies widely between individuals. This is a motility mechanism, distinct from the fermentation-and-gas route behind FODMAPs — caffeine does not need to be a fermentable carbohydrate to provoke symptoms; it acts directly on how briskly the bowel moves.

## Caffeine Relaxes the Lower Esophageal Sphincter — the GERD Route

At the top of the digestive tract, caffeine works through a different mechanism relevant to reflux. Caffeine is associated with relaxation of the lower esophageal sphincter (LES), the valve that normally stays shut to keep stomach acid down; when that valve loosens, acid can move up into the esophagus more easily, which is why caffeinated drinks are frequently named among reflux triggers (NIDDK, MedlinePlus). This is the same LES-relaxation route that fatty meals, alcohol, chocolate, and peppermint act through, and it is entirely separate from the colonic-motility effect that matters for IBS. The practical consequence is that one caffeinated drink can be a problem at both ends for someone who has both conditions — stimulating the colon below and loosening the esophageal valve above — for two unrelated reasons.

## Coffee Is More Than Caffeine — Decaf Still Matters

Coffee is not simply a caffeine delivery vehicle; the beverage itself carries other compounds that affect the gut, which is why switching to decaf reduces but does not eliminate its reflux potential. Decaffeinated coffee still shows some ability to promote reflux, indicating that components of coffee beyond caffeine contribute to the effect (NIDDK, MedlinePlus). Coffee is also mildly acidic and is itself a recognized stimulant of colonic activity independent of its caffeine content, so decaf coffee can still prompt a bowel response in sensitive people with IBS. The takeaway is that "just switch to decaf" is a partial lever, not a full off-switch: it lowers the caffeine dose but leaves the other coffee-specific effects in place. Individual tolerance to decaf varies, and some people find it noticeably gentler while others still react.

## Tea and Energy Drinks — Same Caffeine, Different Extras

Tea and energy drinks share caffeine as the common driver of both the colonic-motility and LES-relaxation routes, but they differ in dose and in what rides along. Black and green tea generally carry less caffeine per serving than a strong coffee, which is why some people tolerate tea better, though strongly brewed tea can still deliver a meaningful stimulant load. Energy drinks tend to sit at the high end of the caffeine range and are usually carbonated and sweetened; the carbonation adds gastric distension relevant to reflux, and any high-fructose or polyol sweeteners in them can add a FODMAP load relevant to IBS on top of the caffeine (Monash University FODMAP framework). So across coffee, tea, and energy drinks the caffeine mechanism is shared, but the total gut impact tracks the dose plus whatever else is in the cup.
