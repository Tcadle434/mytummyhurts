# Gentler Choices for Reflux — Lean Protein, Oats, Banana, Melon, and Non-Citrus Vegetables

Some foods tend to be better tolerated in reflux because they avoid the mechanisms that drive it: they are low in fat, not strongly acidic, and do not relax the lower esophageal sphincter (LES). This document covers the gentler-food family for gastroesophageal reflux (GERD). "Gentler" means less likely to trigger on average, not guaranteed for everyone — tolerance is individual.

## Lean, Low-Fat Proteins

Lean proteins prepared without added fat are generally well tolerated because fat is a key reflux driver: it slows gastric emptying and relaxes the LES. Skinless poultry, white fish, and other lean cuts that are baked, grilled, steamed, or roasted — rather than fried or served in cream sauce — keep the fat load of a meal low and are common "safe base" choices for GERD. The preparation matters as much as the protein: the same chicken breast is gentler grilled than deep-fried. Keeping portions moderate helps too, since even a low-fat meal can provoke reflux if the serving is very large and distends the stomach.

## Oats and Non-Acidic Starches

Oatmeal is a frequently recommended gentle breakfast for reflux: it is low in fat, not acidic, and filling without a large fat load. Other plain, non-acidic starches — rice, potatoes, and plain whole grains — are usually well tolerated on the same logic and form a reliable base for meals. As with all reflux foods, how they are dressed matters: buttery, creamy, or fried preparations reintroduce the fat that oats and rice otherwise help avoid. Served plainly and in reasonable portions, these starches sidestep the acid-exposure and sphincter-relaxation pathways that trigger heartburn.

## Bananas, Melons, and Non-Citrus Vegetables

Bananas and melons (such as cantaloupe and honeydew) are low-acid fruits that are commonly better tolerated than citrus or tomato, making them frequent choices for people avoiding acidic triggers. Most non-citrus, non-tomato vegetables — leafy greens, green beans, carrots, cucumber, broccoli, and similar — are low in fat and not strongly acidic, so they tend to be gentle when prepared without heavy fat, cream, or frying. These are "usually better tolerated" foods rather than guaranteed-safe ones: reflux response is individually variable, and the surrounding meal (its fat, size, and timing) still shapes whether even gentle foods sit well.
