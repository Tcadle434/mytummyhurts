# Polyols — Sorbitol, Mannitol, and Sugar-Free Sweeteners

Polyols are the "P" in FODMAP — sugar alcohols such as sorbitol and mannitol that occur naturally in some fruits and vegetables and are added to many sugar-free products. In the Monash University framework they are poorly absorbed carbohydrates that can provoke IBS symptoms.

## How Polyols Trigger Symptoms

Polyols (sugar alcohols) are absorbed slowly and incompletely in the small intestine, and the larger molecules cannot readily cross the gut wall at all. According to the Monash University FODMAP framework, the unabsorbed portion stays in the gut lumen, where it exerts an osmotic pull that draws water in and is then fermented by bacteria in the large intestine. Both effects — the extra water and the fermentation gas — can produce bloating, cramping, wind, and a laxative, loosening effect on the stool. The two polyols most relevant to whole foods are sorbitol and mannitol; others such as maltitol, xylitol, and erythritol appear mainly as added sweeteners. Because absorption is limited, polyols follow the same dose-related pattern as other FODMAPs: the larger the serving, the more unabsorbed polyol reaches the bowel.

## Sorbitol in Stone Fruit and Apples

Sorbitol occurs naturally in a group of fruits that includes stone fruits — apricots, peaches, plums, nectarines, and cherries — along with apples and pears. Monash University identifies these as sorbitol sources, which means some of them carry two FODMAP concerns at once: apples and pears are high in both excess fructose and sorbitol, stacking two mechanisms in a single fruit. Prunes (dried plums) are a familiar example of sorbitol's osmotic, stool-loosening effect, and drying concentrates the sorbitol into a small serving. Blackberries and lychees are also commonly cited sorbitol-containing fruits. As with all polyols, tolerance is tied to portion, so a few pieces may sit differently from a large bowl of the same fruit.

## Mannitol in Mushrooms and Cauliflower

Mannitol is the polyol found on the vegetable side. Mushrooms and cauliflower are the best-known mannitol sources in the Monash University framework, with celery, snow peas, and sweet potato (in larger servings) also contributing. This is a useful fact because these are savoury, everyday vegetables that people rarely suspect, unlike obviously sweet foods. The mannitol mechanism is the same osmotic-plus-fermentation effect as sorbitol. Preparation offers little escape here: mannitol is not destroyed by ordinary cooking, so sautéed mushrooms carry mannitol just as raw ones do. Some mushroom types (such as certain canned or oyster mushrooms) are noted by Monash as lower in mannitol, but standard button, cremini, and portobello mushrooms are treated as mannitol-rich.

## Sugar-Free Gum, Mints, and "Diet" Products

Polyols are manufactured on a large scale as low-calorie sweeteners, and this is where the biggest concentrated doses often hide. Sugar-free chewing gum, mints, and confectionery are commonly sweetened with sorbitol, mannitol, xylitol, maltitol, isomalt, or similar polyols — recognisable on ingredient lists by names ending in "-ol." Because these products deliver polyol without the bulk of a whole food, even a few pieces of gum or several mints can add up to a meaningful dose, and the osmotic laxative effect of added polyols is well documented. "Sugar-free," "no added sugar," and "diet" labels are the practical cue to check for polyol sweeteners. Within the FODMAP framework these added polyols behave exactly like the naturally occurring ones — the same osmotic and fermentation mechanism, just in a more concentrated and easily overlooked form.
