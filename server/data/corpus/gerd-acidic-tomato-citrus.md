# Acidic Foods — Tomato, Citrus, and Esophageal Acid Exposure

Acidic foods such as tomato products and citrus are traditionally listed as reflux triggers. The evidence here is more individual and less mechanistically clean than for fat or large meals, and honest guidance says so. This document covers the acidic-food family for gastroesophageal reflux (GERD).

## Tomato and Tomato-Based Sauces

Tomatoes and concentrated tomato products — marinara, pasta and pizza sauce, ketchup, tomato paste, and tomato soup — are acidic and are frequently reported to aggravate heartburn and regurgitation in people with GERD. Cooking and concentrating tomatoes (as in a long-simmered sauce) raises the acid per serving, and these dishes are often also higher in fat, which compounds the effect. NIDDK and MedlinePlus commonly list tomato-based foods among items people with reflux may find worsen symptoms. The mechanism usually described is increased acid exposure of an already-sensitive esophagus rather than a change to the sphincter itself. Because tomato triggers vary considerably between individuals, tomato dishes are a moderate rather than universal reflux flag.

## Citrus Fruits and Juices

Oranges, grapefruit, lemons, limes, and their juices are acidic and are commonly named as reflux aggravators. The proposed effect is direct acidic irritation of esophageal tissue that is already exposed to stomach acid, intensifying the perception of heartburn rather than necessarily causing more reflux events. Citrus juice on an empty stomach is a frequently reported trigger. It is worth being precise: dietary acids like citric acid are far weaker than stomach acid, so the response is thought to reflect sensitivity of irritated tissue and differs a lot from person to person. Some people with GERD tolerate citrus without trouble, which is why it is a personal trigger to test rather than a rule.

## Why the Evidence Is Softer Here

For fat and large late meals, the mechanisms (delayed emptying, sphincter relaxation, gastric distension) are well characterized. For acidic foods, the picture is weaker and more variable: much of the support comes from symptom questionnaires and individual reports rather than from strong mechanistic data, and controlled evidence that removing acidic foods reliably reduces reflux is limited. This nuance is a feature, not a gap — treating tomato and citrus as "test for yourself" triggers, rather than certain ones, matches what the evidence actually supports. Someone who never reacts to marinara should not assume they must avoid it.
