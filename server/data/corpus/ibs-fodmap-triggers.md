# IBS and High-FODMAP Trigger Foods

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) symptoms are frequently provoked by fermentable carbohydrates known as FODMAPs. The low-FODMAP approach, developed at Monash University, identifies the specific carbohydrate groups most likely to trigger bloating, gas, cramping, and altered bowel habits.

## Garlic and Onion (Fructans)

Garlic and onion are among the most common IBS triggers because they are concentrated sources of fructans, a type of oligosaccharide. Fructans are not absorbed in the small intestine and are rapidly fermented by gut bacteria, producing gas and drawing water into the bowel. Even small amounts of garlic or onion — including garlic-infused sauces, onion powder, and stock — can provoke symptoms in sensitive individuals. Garlic-infused oil is generally better tolerated because fructans are water-soluble and do not transfer into oil.

## Alliums and Related Vegetables

The allium family (garlic, onion, leek, shallot, spring onion bulbs) shares the same fructan-related risk. The green tops of spring onions and chives are typically low in FODMAPs and better tolerated than the white bulb.

## Wheat and Rye

Wheat- and rye-based foods (bread, pasta) contribute fructans as well. Sourdough and spelt are sometimes better tolerated. Gluten itself is not the FODMAP; the fructan content is the usual driver of IBS symptoms.

## Generally Gentler Choices

Plain rice, oats, potatoes, carrots, cucumber, and most lean proteins (chicken, fish, eggs) are low in FODMAPs and are usually well tolerated by people with IBS. Portion size still matters — large servings of otherwise-safe foods can accumulate FODMAPs.
