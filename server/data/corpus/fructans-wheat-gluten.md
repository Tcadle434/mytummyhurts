# Fructans in Wheat and Rye — and Why It Is Usually Not the Gluten

Wheat, rye, and barley carry fructans, the same oligosaccharide group found in garlic and onion. In the low-FODMAP framework from Monash University, these grain fructans — not the protein gluten — are the usual driver of IBS-type symptoms from bread and pasta.

## Wheat and Rye Carry Fructans

Wheat- and rye-based staples — bread, pasta, couscous, crackers, and many baked goods — contribute fructans to the diet. Fructans are oligosaccharides that the small intestine cannot absorb; according to the Monash University FODMAP framework, they travel to the large intestine and are fermented by gut bacteria, generating gas and drawing water into the bowel, which can trigger bloating and cramping in people with IBS. Wheat is a notable dietary fructan source not because it is unusually concentrated per gram but because it is eaten so frequently and in such quantity across a typical day. This means the everyday load from bread and pasta can add up quickly even when no single serving feels large.

## The Fructan, Not the Gluten

Gluten is a protein; fructans are carbohydrates. They are entirely different molecules that happen to travel together in wheat, rye, and barley. In the Monash University framework, it is the fructan — a FODMAP — that ferments in the gut and produces IBS-type symptoms, whereas gluten itself is not a FODMAP and is not fermented in that way. This distinction matters because people who feel better avoiding wheat often assume gluten is the problem, when for many the relief comes from removing the fructan load. Coeliac disease is a separate, immune-mediated condition in which gluten must be avoided strictly for medical reasons; that is different from the fructan-driven digestive discomfort described here. This document addresses only the fructan mechanism, not coeliac disease.

## Sourdough, Spelt, and Slow Fermentation

Traditional sourdough bread is often better tolerated than standard bread, and the mechanism points back to fructans. In a long sourdough fermentation, the wild yeasts and lactic-acid bacteria consume some of the fructans in the dough before baking, so the finished loaf can carry a lower fructan load. Monash University notes that traditionally fermented sourdough — particularly spelt sourdough — can be lower in FODMAPs than conventionally leavened bread. The emphasis is on genuine slow fermentation; breads that merely add sour flavouring without a long ferment do not get the same fructan reduction. Spelt, an ancient wheat relative, is likewise sometimes better tolerated than modern wheat, again reflecting differences in fructan content rather than any absence of gluten.

## Lower-Fructan Grain Choices

Not all grains carry a meaningful fructan load. In the Monash University framework, plain rice and oats are low-FODMAP staples, and naturally gluten-free grains and pseudocereals such as quinoa, buckwheat, corn (maize), and millet are commonly used as lower-fructan alternatives to wheat and rye. Choosing these does not require avoiding gluten for its own sake — the benefit for a fructan-sensitive person comes from the lower oligosaccharide content. Portion still matters, because even lower-FODMAP grains can accumulate FODMAPs in very large servings, and some processed gluten-free products add other high-FODMAP ingredients. Checking that a substitute is genuinely low in fructans, rather than simply gluten-free, is the practical point.
