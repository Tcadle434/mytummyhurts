# Gluten vs. Fructan — What Actually Drives Wheat Symptoms in NCGS

Wheat, rye, and barley carry two different things that often get blamed together: gluten, a protein, and fructans, a fermentable carbohydrate in the oligosaccharide ("O") group of FODMAPs. In people who report non-celiac gluten sensitivity (NCGS) alongside IBS-type symptoms, the low-FODMAP research from Monash University points to the fructan — not the gluten — as the usual driver. This document separates those two components and, critically, keeps celiac disease apart from both: celiac is a distinct autoimmune condition, not a FODMAP problem, and the distinction changes what the food actually does in the body.

## Gluten and Fructans Are Two Different Things in Wheat

A slice of wheat bread delivers gluten (the elastic protein network that gives dough its stretch) and fructans (short chains of fructose the small intestine cannot absorb) at the same time. Because they arrive together, a person who feels worse after bread naturally blames "gluten" — the word on the label. But the two components act by completely different routes. Gluten is a protein handled by the immune system and digestive enzymes; fructans are a carbohydrate that passes undigested into the large intestine, where gut bacteria ferment them and produce gas while the fructans draw water into the bowel osmotically (Monash University FODMAP framework). That fermentation-and-water mechanism is the same one behind bloating and cramping from garlic and onion, which contain no gluten at all — a hint that in many cases the carbohydrate, not the protein, is doing the work.

## Blinded Studies Point to the Fructan, Not the Gluten

The strongest reason to separate gluten from fructan in NCGS is that when researchers remove the expectation and test the two components separately under blinded conditions, the fructan tends to reproduce IBS-type symptoms while purified gluten often does not. Monash University, whose team has run this kind of re-challenge work, describes the fructan content of wheat as the more likely trigger of gut symptoms in people who believe they are gluten-sensitive but do not have celiac disease. The practical read is that many self-identified "gluten reactions" in the IBS population track the FODMAP load of the food rather than the protein. Evidence here is still developing and individual responses vary, but the direction is consistent: for gut symptoms in NCGS, the oligosaccharide is the usual suspect. This is why a low-FODMAP approach frequently eases symptoms even without a strictly gluten-free diet.

## Celiac Disease Is a Separate Condition — Do Not Conflate It

Celiac disease is not a FODMAP problem and must not be lumped in with NCGS. It is an autoimmune condition in which gluten specifically triggers the immune system to damage the lining of the small intestine (MedlinePlus, NIDDK). For someone with celiac disease, the issue is unambiguously the gluten protein, even in tiny amounts, and strict lifelong gluten avoidance is the medically established response — a low-FODMAP swap does not address it. This is the opposite of the NCGS picture, where reducing the fructan load often helps and trace gluten is not the driver. The two should never be treated as the same thing: confusing them risks under-treating a genuine autoimmune disease or needlessly over-restricting someone whose real trigger is a fermentable carbohydrate. Diagnosis of celiac disease is a clinical matter, not something a food-fact framework decides.

## Sourdough, Spelt, and Portion — Why Some Wheat Foods Sit Easier

Within the fructan mechanism, not all wheat foods carry the same load, which is why some feel more tolerable without being gluten-free. Traditional long-fermented sourdough gives its bacteria and yeast extended time to consume some of the fructans in the flour before baking, so a properly fermented sourdough can end up lower in FODMAPs than standard quick-leavened bread (Monash University). Spelt, an ancient wheat, is also sometimes better tolerated, and again this is about fructan content rather than the presence or absence of gluten — spelt still contains gluten. Portion matters too, because fructans stack: a small serving of pasta may stay under a person's threshold while a large bowl pushes past it. None of these levers help with celiac disease, where the protein itself is the problem regardless of fermentation, grain variety, or serving size.
