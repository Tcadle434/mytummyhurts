# Meal Size and Timing — Large Meals, Late Meals, and Lying Down

How much and when a person eats are among the most consistently supported reflux factors — often more reliable than any single "trigger food." This document covers the meal-size-and-timing mechanism family for gastroesophageal reflux (GERD). Unlike some food triggers, the effects here are well characterized.

## Large Meals and Gastric Distension

Large meals distend the stomach, and a fuller, more stretched stomach both raises pressure against the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) and promotes transient relaxations of that valve, making reflux more likely. A big portion also takes longer to empty, so acid and food remain available to reflux for longer after eating. This is why the same food can be tolerated in a small serving but trigger heartburn in a large one — the volume of the meal is doing much of the work. Guidance for GERD from NIDDK and MedlinePlus commonly recommends smaller, more frequent meals in place of large ones, precisely because reducing meal volume reduces distension and the pressure on the sphincter.

## Late Meals and Lying Down

Eating shortly before lying down is a strong, well-supported reflux contributor. When a person is upright, gravity helps keep stomach contents down; when they lie flat with a full stomach, that assistance is lost and acid can move into the esophagus far more easily. Late dinners, bedtime snacks, and lying down or napping soon after eating are therefore classic triggers for nighttime heartburn. Common guidance is to leave a gap of about two to three hours between the last meal and lying down, and elevating the head of the bed is often suggested to keep gravity working overnight. The combination of a large, late meal followed by reclining is one of the most reliable ways to provoke reflux.

## Why Timing Beats Guessing at Foods

For meal size and timing, the mechanisms — distension, delayed emptying, and loss of gravity when reclining — are well established, which makes these among the most dependable levers in reflux, more so than uncertain food triggers like spicy or acidic items. Portion and timing also interact with trigger foods: a fatty or large meal eaten late and followed by lying down stacks several mechanisms at once. This is why "smaller and earlier" is emphasized in reflux guidance, and why total meal size and timing often deserve more attention than hunting for a single culprit ingredient.
