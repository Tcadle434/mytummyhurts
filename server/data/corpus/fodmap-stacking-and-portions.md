# FODMAP Stacking and Portion Size — Why the Dose Matters

The single most important idea in the low-FODMAP framework from Monash University is that FODMAPs are cumulative. A food is rarely "safe" or "unsafe" in absolute terms; what matters is the total FODMAP load reaching the gut and whether it crosses an individual's threshold.

## FODMAPs Add Up Across a Meal

FODMAPs are dose-dependent, and their effects accumulate. Monash University describes the total FODMAP load as additive: the fructans, GOS, excess fructose, lactose, and polyols from everything eaten in one sitting combine, and it is the combined amount arriving in the gut that determines whether symptoms appear. This is why a person can tolerate any one FODMAP food on its own yet react when several are eaten together — a sandwich with wheat bread (fructans), a side of beans (GOS), an apple (fructose and sorbitol), and a milky coffee (lactose) may each be modest alone but stack into a large load together. The osmotic water-drawing and bacterial fermentation that drive IBS symptoms scale with that combined dose, so managing the total, not just avoiding a single culprit, is the central skill.

## The Individual Threshold

Each person has their own tolerance level — a threshold below which a given FODMAP load causes little trouble and above which symptoms emerge. Monash University frames low-FODMAP eating around staying under that personal threshold rather than eliminating FODMAPs entirely. Thresholds vary between people and can even vary for the same person depending on gut sensitivity at the time, so there is no universal cut-off that applies to everyone. This individuality is why the framework distinguishes "how much" from "whether": the practical question is not simply *is this food high in FODMAPs* but *does this portion, added to everything else, stay under my limit*. It also explains why blanket avoidance is often unnecessary — many high-FODMAP foods fit in small enough servings.

## Portion Size Changes the Verdict

Because FODMAPs are dose-related, serving size can move a food from tolerable to symptomatic. Many foods that are low-FODMAP in a small portion become high-FODMAP in a larger one, as the FODMAP simply accumulates with quantity — Monash University assigns foods a serving guide precisely because the answer depends on amount. A small handful of a given nut, a modest serving of certain legumes, or a limited portion of a borderline vegetable may sit comfortably, while doubling the serving pushes the same food over the line. The corollary is that "this food is fine" and "this food is a problem" can both be true statements about the same ingredient at different portion sizes. Treating portion as part of the food's identity, rather than an afterthought, is what makes the framework work.

## Even Gentle Foods Can Accumulate

Low-FODMAP does not mean zero-FODMAP, and even reliably gentle foods can contribute to the total in large quantities. Monash University notes that some foods rated low-FODMAP at a normal serving still carry small amounts of FODMAPs that add up if the portion is very large or if many such foods are combined. This means a plate built entirely from "safe" ingredients can still reach a meaningful load if every component is oversized. The practical takeaway is spacing and moderation: keeping servings reasonable, not clustering several FODMAP-containing foods into one meal, and allowing time between meals all help keep the cumulative load under the personal threshold. Portion awareness applies across the whole diet, not only to the obvious trigger foods.
