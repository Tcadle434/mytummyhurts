# Excess Fructose — Apples, Pears, Honey, and the Glucose Balance

Fructose is a natural sugar in fruit, honey, and some sweeteners. In the low-FODMAP framework from Monash University, the concern is not fructose as such but *excess* fructose — fructose present in greater amount than the glucose alongside it. This glucose balance is the key idea that decides whether a sweet food is a FODMAP problem.

## The Glucose Balance Concept

Fructose is absorbed in the small intestine, but its absorption is slow and limited unless glucose is present to help carry it across the gut wall. Monash University frames the relevant "F" in FODMAP as fructose *in excess of glucose*: when a food contains roughly equal or more glucose than fructose, the fructose tends to be absorbed and causes no trouble, but when it contains substantially more fructose than glucose, the surplus fructose goes unabsorbed. That leftover fructose then behaves like other FODMAPs — it draws water into the small intestine osmotically and is fermented by bacteria further along — producing bloating, wind, and loose stools in sensitive people. This is why two equally sweet foods can differ sharply: what matters is the ratio, not the total sugar.

## High-Fructose Fruits: Apple, Pear, Mango

Apples, pears, and mangoes are the fruits most often flagged for excess fructose, because in each the fructose content clearly outweighs the glucose. Under the Monash University framework this surplus fructose is the mechanism that can trigger IBS-type symptoms after these fruits, independent of how "healthy" the fruit is in other respects. Watermelon and dried fruits are also commonly cited as higher-fructose choices, with drying compounding the effect by concentrating the sugars into a small serving. The practical consequence is that these fruits tend to be higher-risk in ordinary portions, whereas fruits with a more even glucose-to-fructose balance are generally gentler on the same gut.

## Honey, Agave, and Added Sweeteners

Honey and agave nectar are notable excess-fructose sweeteners: both are naturally high in fructose relative to glucose, so they carry the same unabsorbed-fructose mechanism as high-fructose fruits and are treated as high-FODMAP by Monash University. High-fructose corn syrup, widely used in soft drinks and processed foods, likewise skews toward fructose. Table sugar (sucrose) is handled differently — it is made of glucose and fructose bound in equal measure, so the glucose released alongside the fructose assists its absorption, and sucrose is generally better tolerated than an equal sweetness from honey or agave. This contrast is a clear illustration that the glucose balance, not sweetness, governs the FODMAP risk.

## Fruit Juice and Concentrated Forms

Juicing removes a fruit's fibre and delivers the sugars of several pieces of fruit in one easy-to-drink glass, so fruit juice concentrates fructose into a larger single dose than a person would usually eat whole. Even juice from a fruit with a fair glucose-fructose balance can present a bigger fructose load than the whole fruit, and juice or concentrate made from high-fructose fruits like apple or pear is higher still. Dried fruit works the same way through water removal, packing the sugars of a large portion into a small handful. In the Monash University framework these concentrated forms are more likely to exceed an individual's fructose tolerance than the equivalent whole fruit eaten in a normal serving.
