# Spicy Foods and Capsaicin — Esophageal Irritation and High Individual Variation

Spicy foods are widely listed as reflux triggers, but this is one of the more personal and less mechanistically settled categories. This document covers the spicy/capsaicin family for gastroesophageal reflux (GERD), and is honest that the evidence here is weaker and more individual than for fat or large meals.

## Capsaicin and the Esophageal Lining

Capsaicin is the pungent compound in chili peppers, hot sauce, and cayenne. In reflux, the mechanism usually described is direct irritation and heightened sensitivity of the esophageal lining rather than a change to the sphincter or acid volume. Capsaicin acts on sensory receptors involved in the perception of burning and pain, which can make heartburn feel more intense in tissue that is already exposed to stomach acid. This is why a spicy meal can amplify the sensation of reflux even when it is not clearly causing more reflux events. Dietary guidance for GERD from NIDDK and MedlinePlus commonly notes that spicy foods worsen symptoms for many people, while framing them as a personal trigger to identify.

## Very High Individual Variation

Spice tolerance in reflux differs dramatically from person to person — more so than for most other triggers. Many people with GERD find chili, hot sauce, curries, and heavily peppered dishes aggravate heartburn, yet others eat spicy food regularly without trouble, and populations with spice-heavy cuisines do not uniformly report worse reflux. The controlled evidence that spicy food reliably worsens reflux is mixed, and part of what people experience may be irritation and expectation rather than increased acid exposure. The practical takeaway is that spice belongs in the "test for yourself" bucket: if peppery or chili-rich dishes reproducibly bring on symptoms for a given person, that is meaningful for them, but spice should not be treated as a universal reflux rule.
