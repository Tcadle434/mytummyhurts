# Fructans — Garlic, Onion, and the Allium Family

Garlic and onion are among the foods most commonly linked to IBS symptoms in the low-FODMAP framework developed at Monash University, because they are concentrated sources of fructans — a chain-form carbohydrate in the oligosaccharide ("O") group of FODMAPs.

## Why Garlic and Onion Provoke Symptoms

Garlic and onion concentrate fructans, a type of oligosaccharide the human small intestine cannot break down or absorb. According to the Monash University FODMAP framework, undigested fructans pass into the large intestine, where resident bacteria ferment them rapidly. That fermentation releases gas, and the fructans also exert an osmotic effect that draws water into the bowel. The combination can produce the bloating, distension, wind, and cramping that people with IBS often report. Because fructans are so concentrated in these two vegetables, even modest culinary amounts can push a sensitive person past the point where symptoms appear — which is why garlic and onion are treated as high-FODMAP ingredients rather than incidental seasonings.

## Onion Powder, Garlic Salt, and Stock

Drying and grinding an onion or a garlic clove does not remove its fructans. Onion powder, garlic powder, garlic salt, dehydrated onion flakes, and onion- or garlic-based stock cubes and broths therefore remain high-FODMAP within the Monash University framework, and are common hidden sources in sauces, marinades, spice blends, gravies, soups, and packaged seasonings. Because these ingredients are dispersed evenly through a dish, the fructan load is easy to overlook even when no whole clove or bulb is visible. Products labelled with "natural flavours," "vegetable powder," or simply "spices" can also carry onion or garlic. Reading ingredient lists is the practical way to spot this fructan source, since the amounts used in commercial products are often enough to matter for sensitive individuals.

## Garlic-Infused Oil — Why It Is Different

Fructans are water-soluble and are not soluble in fat or oil. This single property explains why garlic-infused oil behaves differently from garlic itself. When garlic is steeped in oil, its flavour compounds — which are oil-soluble — migrate into the oil, but the fructans, being water-soluble, largely stay behind in the solids rather than dissolving into the fat. Monash University identifies garlic-infused oil (with the solids removed) as low-FODMAP, making it a widely used way to capture garlic flavour without the fructan load. The same logic applies to infusing oil with onion. Note the mechanism depends on the fructans not entering the oil; simmering whole garlic in a water-based sauce and leaving it in does not achieve the same result, because there the fructans dissolve into the watery liquid.

## The Allium Family and Its Gentler Parts

The allium family — garlic, brown and red onion, shallot, leek, and spring onion (scallion) — shares the same fructan-related mechanism, but the fructans are not evenly distributed within each plant. In the Monash University framework, the green leafy tops of spring onions and chives are low in fructans and are commonly used as a low-FODMAP substitute for the flavour of onion, whereas the white bulb of the spring onion, like the leek bulb, is higher. This within-plant variation means a cook can often keep an allium note in a dish by using the green portions while setting aside the bulb. Asafoetida (hing), a resin spice, is another commonly cited way to echo the savoury allium character without the fructan load.
