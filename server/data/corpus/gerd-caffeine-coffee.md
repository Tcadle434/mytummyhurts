# Caffeine and Coffee — Sphincter Effects in Reflux, and the Decaf Nuance

Coffee and caffeine are common heartburn triggers, and the reasons involve both the caffeine itself and other compounds in coffee. This document covers the caffeine/coffee family for gastroesophageal reflux (GERD). As with other reflux triggers, tolerance varies from person to person.

## Caffeine and the Lower Esophageal Sphincter

Caffeine is associated with lowered pressure at the lower esophageal sphincter (LES), the muscular valve that normally keeps stomach acid from rising into the esophagus. When that valve relaxes, reflux is easier, which is the usual explanation for why caffeinated drinks can bring on heartburn. Reflux literature and dietary guidance from bodies such as NIDDK and MedlinePlus commonly list caffeine-containing beverages among reflux triggers. Caffeine appears in coffee, tea, cola, and energy drinks, so the effect is not unique to coffee. Because the response depends on the amount consumed and on individual sensitivity, some people notice reflux only after a large or late-day dose.

## Coffee Is More Than Caffeine

Coffee can provoke reflux through pathways beyond caffeine alone. Coffee also stimulates gastric acid secretion, and compounds in the brew — not only the caffeine — contribute to this effect. That is why some people react to coffee more than to an equal dose of caffeine from another source. The roast, brew method, and volume all influence how much a given cup affects a person. In practice, a large mug of strong coffee, especially on an empty stomach or close to bedtime, is a frequently reported reflux trigger. The combined LES-relaxing and acid-stimulating actions make coffee a common heartburn contributor for people with GERD.

## The Decaf Nuance

Decaffeinated coffee tends to provoke less reflux than regular coffee, which points to caffeine as one active driver — but decaf is not automatically trouble-free. Because non-caffeine components of coffee also stimulate acid, some people still experience heartburn with decaf, just often to a lesser degree. Honest guidance is that switching to decaf may reduce symptoms for some individuals without eliminating them, and it is worth testing personally rather than assumed. This individual variability is the key point: coffee is a common trigger, decaf is usually gentler, and neither is universal.
