# Alcohol and the Gut — One Drink, Several Mechanisms Across IBS and Reflux

Alcohol is a cross-condition trigger: the same drink can act through more than one route at once, and the route differs by beverage. Alcohol itself relaxes the lower esophageal sphincter and can irritate the gut lining, which matters for both GERD and IBS. On top of that base effect, beer carries fructans and carbonation, wine carries histamine and acidity, and mixed drinks add sugar and FODMAP-bearing mixers. This document maps those mechanisms so a beer, a glass of wine, and a cocktail can be understood as genuinely different exposures rather than interchangeable "alcohol."

## Alcohol Itself Relaxes the Valve and Irritates the Gut

Independent of what it is dissolved in, ethanol has two effects that show up across conditions. First, alcohol is associated with relaxation of the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) — the muscular valve that normally keeps stomach contents down — which makes it easier for acid to reflux upward and is why alcoholic drinks are commonly listed among reflux triggers (NIDDK, MedlinePlus). Second, alcohol can irritate the digestive tract lining and influence gut motility and water handling, which is why some people with IBS notice looser or more urgent stools and more cramping after drinking. Because these are properties of ethanol itself, they apply in some degree to any alcoholic drink; the beverage-specific components below stack on top of this shared base rather than replacing it.

## Beer — Fructans Plus Carbonation

Beer is a double hit within the FODMAP framework and the reflux framework at once. It is brewed from grains such as wheat and barley, so it can carry fructans — the same fermentable oligosaccharides found in wheat bread, garlic, and onion — which pass to the large intestine, get fermented by gut bacteria, and produce gas and osmotic water movement that can drive bloating and cramping in IBS (Monash University FODMAP framework). Beer is also carbonated, and the swallowed gas distends the stomach; that gastric distension can increase pressure on the lower esophageal sphincter and promote reflux. So a beer combines a fermentable-carbohydrate load with mechanical distension, plus the shared ethanol LES effect — three routes to symptoms in a single glass, which is why it is often reported as harder to tolerate than an equal amount of a low-FODMAP spirit.

## Wine — Histamine Plus Acidity

Wine acts through a different pair of mechanisms than beer. As a fermented product, wine can contain histamine and other biogenic amines, and in people with histamine intolerance — where the body clears histamine poorly — histamine-rich drinks are commonly cited among the triggers for flushing, headache, and gut upset (RPAH Allergy Unit food-intolerance work). Wine is also acidic, and that acidity, combined with alcohol's relaxing effect on the lower esophageal sphincter, makes wine a frequent reflux trigger for people with GERD. Red wines are often singled out anecdotally, but both red and white are acidic and both are fermented. As with all of these drinks, responses vary from person to person; the point is that wine's profile is histamine-and-acid, distinct from beer's fructan-and-carbonation profile even though both contain alcohol.

## Cocktails and Mixers — The Sugar and FODMAP Add-Ons

With mixed drinks, the base spirit is usually the smaller part of the FODMAP story; the mixer often matters more. Many cocktails are built on fruit juices, sodas, syrups, and sweeteners that carry their own load: apple or pear juice and agave or honey syrups can be high in excess fructose or polyols, and non-diet sodas add a sugar load, while the carbonation in tonic, soda, and cola contributes gastric distension relevant to reflux (Monash University FODMAP framework). A rum-and-cola or a fruity punch therefore layers mixer FODMAPs and carbonation onto the shared ethanol LES effect. A plain spirit over ice, by contrast, carries mainly the ethanol effect without the added fructans of beer, the histamine of wine, or the sugar of a sweet mixer — a useful way to see why "a drink" is not one fixed exposure.
