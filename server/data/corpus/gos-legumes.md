# GOS — Beans, Lentils, Chickpeas, and Soy

Legumes are the classic dietary source of galacto-oligosaccharides (GOS), an oligosaccharide group in the FODMAP framework from Monash University. GOS is the reason beans have a reputation for causing gas, and it behaves much like the fructans found in wheat and onion.

## What GOS Is and Why Legumes Cause Gas

Galacto-oligosaccharides (GOS) are short carbohydrate chains found in beans, lentils, chickpeas, and soybeans. Humans lack the enzyme needed to break the bonds in GOS, so — as Monash University describes for the oligosaccharide group — it passes undigested into the large intestine, where gut bacteria ferment it. That fermentation produces gas and draws water into the bowel, which is the well-known reason legumes can cause wind, bloating, and abdominal discomfort, and why GOS marks many legumes as high-FODMAP. Because GOS is a FODMAP, the response is dose-related: small portions of some legumes may be tolerated by many people, while larger servings raise the GOS load and the likelihood of symptoms.

## Canned and Rinsed vs Dried

How a legume is prepared changes its GOS load. GOS is water-soluble, so when legumes are canned in liquid, some of the GOS leaches out of the beans and into the surrounding brine. Draining and thoroughly rinsing canned legumes therefore discards a portion of the GOS along with the liquid, and Monash University notes that canned, rinsed legumes tend to be lower in FODMAPs than the same legume cooked from dried. This is why canned-and-rinsed chickpeas or lentils are often better tolerated in a modest serving than a large helping of home-cooked dried beans. The practical steps are to tip the beans into a sieve, discard the canning liquid (aquafaba included, as it is rich in the leached oligosaccharides), and rinse under running water before use.

## Lentils and Chickpeas in Small Portions

Lentils and chickpeas are not strictly off-limits in the low-FODMAP framework — their tolerability is largely a matter of portion, because GOS accumulates with the amount eaten. Monash University indicates that a small serving of canned, rinsed lentils or chickpeas can be low enough in FODMAPs to suit many people, whereas a large serving pushes the GOS load higher and is more likely to trigger symptoms. This portion sensitivity makes legumes a good example of FODMAP stacking within a single food: the same chickpea is gentle in a spoonful and provoking by the cupful. Combining canned-and-rinsed preparation with a controlled serving size is the usual way people keep legumes in the diet.

## Tofu, Tempeh, and Soy Forms

Soy illustrates how processing redistributes GOS. Whole and minimally processed soy — soybeans and soy milk made from whole beans — retains GOS and tends to be higher in FODMAPs. Firm tofu is different: it is made by coagulating soy protein and pressing out the watery whey, and because GOS is water-soluble, much of it drains away with that liquid. Monash University identifies firm, drained tofu as low-FODMAP, while silken tofu, which is not pressed and retains its liquid, is higher. Tempeh, made from fermented soybeans, is also commonly better tolerated, as fermentation can reduce the oligosaccharide content. The pattern mirrors the canning rule: steps that remove water tend to remove GOS.
