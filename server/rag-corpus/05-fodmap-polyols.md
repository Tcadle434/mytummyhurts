# Polyols — Sorbitol, Mannitol, and Sugar Alcohols

Polyols, or sugar alcohols, are the "P" in FODMAP and include sorbitol, mannitol, xylitol, maltitol, isomalt, and erythritol. They are absorbed slowly and only partially across the wall of the small intestine, so a meaningful fraction of any polyol eaten remains in the gut lumen, where it draws in water osmotically and is fermented by colonic bacteria. At higher doses this combination produces not merely bloating and gas but a frank laxative effect — which is precisely why several polyols are marketed commercially as laxatives and why "sugar-free" products carry a laxative warning. Polyols occur naturally in certain fruits and vegetables and are added, in far larger and more concentrated amounts, to sugar-free foods, so both natural and manufactured sources need to be considered (Monash University FODMAP).

## Sorbitol in fruit

Sorbitol is the polyol found in stone fruits — apricots, peaches, plums, nectarines, and cherries — as well as in apples, pears, blackberries, and large servings of avocado. A particularly troublesome feature is that sorbitol frequently co-occurs with excess fructose in the very same fruit: apples and pears carry both a fructose imbalance and sorbitol at once, so they deliver a double FODMAP hit that compounds symptoms and makes them among the most reliable fruit triggers. This overlap is why apples and pears appear on caution lists more often than their sugar content alone would suggest, and why even a modest serving can provoke symptoms in a sensitive person when a glucose-balanced fruit of similar size would not.

## Mannitol in vegetables

Mannitol is the polyol most associated with vegetables, and its main dietary sources are mushrooms, cauliflower, celery, snow peas, and large servings of sweet potato. Because mannitol is water-soluble, its behaviour in cooking mirrors that of fructans: slicing mushrooms into a soup, stew, risotto, or sauce spreads the mannitol out of the pieces and throughout the liquid, so the whole dish carries the load rather than just the visible mushroom. Mushrooms and cauliflower are among the most common vegetable polyol triggers, and both are frequently eaten in generous amounts, which pushes the mannitol dose well past threshold. Portion control and choosing lower-mannitol vegetables are the practical levers here.

## Sugar-free products: the hidden dose

The largest and most concentrated polyol exposures usually come not from produce but from manufactured "sugar-free" and "no added sugar" products, where sorbitol, mannitol, xylitol, maltitol, isomalt, and erythritol are used as bulk sweeteners. These turn up in chewing gum, mints, diet and "keto" sweets, chewable vitamins, cough drops, sugar-free chocolate, and protein bars. This is a common and easily missed trigger: chewing several pieces of sugar-free gum through a day — each sweetened with sorbitol or maltitol — can quietly add up to a laxative dose without any actual food being eaten. Erythritol is the best-tolerated polyol because it is largely absorbed in the small intestine and excreted in urine rather than fermented, but sorbitol, mannitol, maltitol, and isomalt ferment readily and cause the most symptoms.

## The osmotic and laxative threshold

Polyols affect even people without IBS once the dose is high enough, which underlines that this is a physiological effect rather than a purely individual sensitivity. Studies indicate that roughly 10 grams of sorbitol or about 20 grams of mannitol can cause gas, bloating, and diarrhoea in otherwise healthy adults — thresholds low enough that a few sugar-free sweets or a large serving of a high-polyol fruit can reach them. This is the basis for the mandatory food-label warning that "excess consumption may have a laxative effect." For someone with IBS, whose gut is more sensitive to both distension and osmotic load, the symptomatic threshold is typically lower still, so the same portion that mildly loosens a healthy person's stool can cause significant discomfort.

## Dose and stacking

Like every FODMAP group, polyol effects are dose-dependent and additive with the rest of the meal. A stone fruit for dessert (sorbitol plus excess fructose) following a mushroom-and-cauliflower main (mannitol) stacks multiple polyol sources plus a fructose imbalance into one sitting, which can trigger symptoms even though no single component was eaten in a large amount. The corollary is encouraging: small servings of polyol-containing foods are frequently tolerated when large ones are not, so the practical approach is to control portions, avoid pairing several polyol sources in the same meal, and treat sugar-free products — the most concentrated source — with particular caution rather than eliminating whole categories of otherwise-healthy fruit and vegetables.
