# GOS — Legumes, Pulses, and Beans

Galacto-oligosaccharides (GOS) — chiefly the sugars raffinose and stachyose — are the second oligosaccharide FODMAP, sitting alongside fructans under the "O." Humans lack the enzyme alpha-galactosidase needed to cleave them, so GOS travels intact through the small intestine into the colon, where bacteria ferment it and generate gas. This is the precise biochemical reason behind the folk observation that "beans make you gassy," and it applies to everyone — not only people with IBS — though those with a sensitive gut feel it as pain and bloating rather than mild wind. Monash University lists legumes and pulses as the primary dietary GOS source, which is why beans, lentils, and chickpeas rank among the most reliable IBS triggers when eaten in typical portions.

## Beans and lentils

The GOS load rises with bean size and type. Larger beans carry the most: red kidney beans, black beans, cannellini, butter beans, and baked beans are high even in modest servings. Chickpeas and lentils sit in the moderate range and have usable low-FODMAP servings — roughly a quarter cup (about 40 grams) of canned, drained lentils or chickpeas is commonly tolerated, while a full cup is not. Split peas, broad beans, and whole soybeans are high. As with every FODMAP, the response is dose-dependent, so the practical goal is rarely total avoidance but finding the portion of each legume that stays under an individual's threshold. Keeping legume servings small and pairing them with low-FODMAP foods is usually enough.

## Rinsing canned legumes reduces GOS

Because GOS is water-soluble, it steadily leaches out of the beans and into whatever liquid surrounds them. This gives a genuinely useful kitchen lever: draining and thoroughly rinsing canned legumes under running water removes a meaningful fraction of the GOS that has dissolved into the canning liquid. As a result, canned-and-rinsed chickpeas or lentils in a small serving are frequently tolerated when the identical quantity of home-boiled dried beans — cooked and eaten in their own starchy liquid — is not. The practical rule Monash-informed dietitians give is to prefer canned pulses over dried, discard the can liquid entirely, and rinse well before use. It is one of the simplest, highest-yield adjustments for eating legumes with IBS.

## Firm tofu and tempeh versus silken tofu

The water-solubility of GOS also produces a large, counter-intuitive difference within soy foods. Making firm or extra-firm tofu involves pressing out most of the water — and the GOS dissolved in it goes with it — so firm tofu ends up low in FODMAPs and is an excellent low-GOS protein choice. Silken tofu, by contrast, retains its water and therefore its GOS, and soy milk made from whole soybeans is likewise higher. Tempeh, which is fermented, is also low-FODMAP because fermentation breaks down the oligosaccharides. So within a single ingredient — soy — the processing method, not just the plant, determines whether it is a trigger, and firm tofu and tempeh can be eaten freely while silken tofu and whole-bean soy milk are limited.

## Other GOS sources to watch

GOS is not confined to beans, and several non-legume foods catch people out. Cashews and pistachios are notably high in GOS and are very easy to over-eat as a handful-at-a-time snack, so they trigger symptoms that are hard to trace back to "just some nuts." Legume flours concentrate GOS: chickpea (besan/gram) flour and lentil flour appear in falafel, socca, papadums, and many gluten-free products, making these surprising triggers for someone who has cut wheat but not beans. Hummus, being blended chickpeas, is high in GOS at normal dip-sized servings. Reading for bean-derived flours and being cautious with cashews and pistachios closes these common blind spots.

## Portion and building tolerance

GOS tolerance can usually be improved rather than treated as fixed. The core tactics are keeping to small portions of the lower-GOS legumes, always rinsing canned beans, choosing firm tofu and tempeh over silken soy, and spacing legume servings out rather than concentrating them in one meal. Some people also benefit from the digestive-enzyme supplement alpha-galactosidase (sold as Beano), which supplies exactly the enzyme humans lack and can reduce legume-related gas when taken with the first bite. Introducing legumes gradually and in modest amounts lets the gut and its microbiota adapt over time, which matters because legumes are otherwise valuable sources of fibre and plant protein worth keeping in the diet where tolerated.
