# GERD — Acidic and Spicy Foods (Direct Irritants)

Some GERD triggers act not by relaxing the lower oesophageal sphincter but by directly irritating an oesophagus whose lining is already inflamed, or by heightening the brain's perception of reflux symptoms. The evidence for acidic and spicy foods is largely observational and highly individual, so — even more than the sphincter-relaxing foods — these belong in the category of personalised triggers to confirm rather than universal rules to obey (Kaltenbach et al., 2006; Newberry & Lynch, Am Fam Physician 2019). An honest summary is that acidic and spicy foods clearly bother some people with reflux and have little or no effect on others, and the only way to know which group a given person is in is a careful trial.

## Citrus

Oranges, lemons, limes, grapefruit, and their juices are acidic and are among the foods most frequently reported to provoke heartburn. The mechanistic picture is instructive: citrus does not appear to lower LES pressure, so it does not cause reflux in the way that fat or chocolate might. Instead the effect is thought to be a direct acid load on, and irritation of, an already-sensitised oesophageal lining, together with heightened symptom perception. Citrus juice taken on an empty stomach, when there is no other food to buffer it, is a common and reproducible self-reported provocation. For people who notice this pattern, reducing straight citrus juice is a reasonable and low-cost thing to test.

## Tomato

Tomatoes and tomato-based products — marinara and pasta sauces, ketchup, pizza sauce, salsa, and tomato paste — are acidic and rank among the most commonly named reflux triggers in patient reports. A relevant practical detail is that cooking and reducing tomatoes concentrates their natural acidity, so a long-simmered, reduced tomato sauce can be considerably more provoking than an equivalent amount of fresh tomato. As with citrus, the plausible mechanism is direct mucosal irritation of a sensitised oesophagus rather than any effect on the sphincter. Someone whose heartburn tracks with tomato-heavy meals can test lighter, less-reduced preparations or smaller portions before concluding that all tomato must be eliminated.

## Vinegar and pickled foods

Vinegar-based dressings, pickles, pickled vegetables, and other sharply acidic condiments add a direct acid load that irritates some individuals with reflux while leaving others unaffected. Because tolerance varies so widely and these foods are usually eaten in small quantities, they are worth testing individually rather than avoiding wholesale. For a person who reacts, the fix is often as simple as moderating the amount of vinegar dressing or choosing a less acidic condiment, rather than removing an entire category of food from the diet.

## Capsaicin and spicy heat

Capsaicin, the compound responsible for the heat of chilli peppers, activates TRPV1 receptors in the gut and can increase both the perception of burning and, in some studies, the oesophagus's sensitivity to acid. The evidence here is genuinely mixed and the response strongly individual: some people find spicy food a dependable trigger for heartburn, while others eat it regularly with no reflux effect at all, and a few studies even explore capsaicin's complex effects on gut sensation over time. For someone whose symptoms clearly follow spicy meals, trial elimination of very hot dishes is sensible; for someone who tolerates them, there is no strong evidence-based reason to remove spice purely as a precaution.

## An honest evidence caveat

Unlike acid-suppressing medication, which has robust trial support, dietary acid restriction in GERD rests on limited controlled evidence, and this should be stated plainly. Part of the reason is that the pH of most "acidic" foods, while low enough to taste sharp, is still far higher than the pH of stomach acid itself, so these foods add little meaningful acid to the stomach — the plausible mechanism is local irritation and heightened symptom perception in an already-sensitised oesophagus, not a change in gastric acidity. The practical upshot is a short, deliberate trial: remove a specific suspected item, watch the personal response, and reintroduce it, rather than assuming on principle that every acidic or spicy food must be permanently avoided.
