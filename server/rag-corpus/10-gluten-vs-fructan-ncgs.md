# Gluten vs Fructan in Non-Celiac Wheat Sensitivity

Many people who do not have celiac disease report feeling better when they avoid wheat, and they commonly attribute that improvement to "non-celiac gluten sensitivity" (NCGS). But a single slice of wheat bread delivers two very different components at the same time: the protein gluten, and the FODMAP carbohydrate fructan. Working out which of the two is actually responsible for a person's symptoms matters a great deal, because the two point to completely different dietary strategies — strict protein avoidance on one hand, versus dose-managed carbohydrate reduction on the other. For years the assumption was simply that gluten was the culprit, but a landmark controlled trial put that assumption to a direct test.

## The Skodje 2018 crossover trial

The question was tested with a rigorous design: a double-blind, placebo-controlled, crossover trial. Adults with self-reported gluten sensitivity, who had been carefully confirmed NOT to have celiac disease, ate concealed muesli bars that contained either isolated gluten, isolated fructan, or a placebo, in randomised order and with neither participants nor researchers knowing which was which. The result was striking and ran against expectation: overall gastrointestinal symptoms, and bloating in particular, were significantly higher after the FRUCTAN bars than after the gluten bars, while gluten itself was not significantly different from placebo (Skodje GI et al., "Fructan, Rather Than Gluten, Induces Symptoms in Patients With Self-Reported Non-Celiac Gluten Sensitivity," Gastroenterology 2018;154:529-539; PMID 29102613).

## What this implies

The implication is that, for a substantial share of people who feel better eating "gluten-free," the active ingredient is most likely the reduction in fructan — a FODMAP effect from cutting down on wheat, and often onion and garlic alongside it — rather than the removal of the gluten protein as such. The underlying gut event is bacterial fermentation of a poorly absorbed carbohydrate, not an immune or protein-driven reaction. This reframes a large fraction of what gets labelled "gluten sensitivity" as, in practice, a FODMAP sensitivity wearing a different name, which in turn changes what such a person actually needs to manage.

## The confounder in self-diagnosis

A crucial complication is that a standard gluten-free diet inevitably removes wheat fructans at the same time as gluten, because both are cut when wheat is cut. This means that anyone who improves on a gluten-free diet cannot tell, from that improvement alone, which of the two components was responsible for their relief. The benefit is real and worth taking seriously, but its cause is genuinely ambiguous without a controlled challenge of the kind Skodje's trial provided. This is the central reason clinicians urge caution about self-diagnosed gluten sensitivity: the observed response does not, by itself, identify gluten as the trigger.

## A practical path

For someone who suspects gluten but has had celiac disease properly excluded by a doctor, the practical takeaway is encouraging. Rather than committing to strict, lifelong, socially difficult gluten avoidance, they can trial reducing their overall fructan load — smaller portions of wheat, plus cutting back on onion and garlic, the other major fructan sources — and see whether that resolves their symptoms. Very often it does, and it leaves the diet far less restrictive than full gluten elimination, since small amounts of wheat may remain comfortably within a personal fructan threshold. Managing a dose is generally easier and more sustainable than avoiding a protein entirely.

## Not the same as celiac disease

It cannot be stressed enough that all of this reasoning applies only to people WITHOUT celiac disease. Celiac disease is a distinct autoimmune condition in which even trace amounts of gluten trigger an immune response that damages the small intestine, and it requires strict, lifelong gluten avoidance regardless of dose or FODMAP considerations. The fructan-versus-gluten distinction is a tool for the large group of people with functional wheat-related symptoms and no autoimmune disease; it is emphatically not a licence for anyone with diagnosed celiac disease to relax their gluten-free diet. Confirming celiac status before applying any of this is essential.
