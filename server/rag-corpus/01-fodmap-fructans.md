# Fructans — Wheat, Rye, Garlic, and Onion

Fructans are chains of fructose molecules with a terminal glucose — the oligosaccharide, the "O" in FODMAP. Because human digestive enzymes cannot break the fructose-to-fructose bonds, fructans pass undigested through the small intestine into the colon, where resident bacteria ferment them into hydrogen and other gases while the sugar osmotically draws water into the bowel. Together these produce the bloating, wind, distension, and cramping that make fructans one of the most common triggers in irritable bowel syndrome. Monash University, the group that developed and validated the low-FODMAP diet, identifies wheat, rye, garlic, and onion as the four major dietary fructan sources, and their food-testing data underpins the serving thresholds used throughout this document.

## Garlic and Onion: the most concentrated source

Garlic and onion are among the most concentrated fructan sources Monash has measured, and even small servings exceed the low-FODMAP threshold — there is no "green" serving of a raw garlic clove or an onion bulb. Onion of every colour (brown, white, red) and garlic in every form provoke symptoms: fresh, dried, roasted, powdered, granulated, or hidden inside stock cubes, bouillon, gravy, and pre-mixed seasoning blends. Garlic and onion powder are especially easy to miss because they appear in spice mixes, marinades, crisps, and most savoury processed foods without obvious pieces. For sensitive individuals this is why "there was only a little onion in the sauce" still reliably triggers a reaction — the fructan dose does not need to be visible to matter.

## Garlic-infused oil and green tops: the safe alternatives

Fructans are water-soluble and do not dissolve into fat, which opens up two reliable low-FODMAP workarounds. Garlic- or onion-infused oil carries the aroma and flavour of the bulb into a dish while leaving the fructans behind in the discarded solids, so Monash classifies infused oils as low-FODMAP. The same chemistry explains a second swap: the green tops of spring onions and scallions, the green parts of leeks, and chives are low in fructans and generally well tolerated, whereas the white bulbs are high and should be avoided. Cooks managing IBS routinely rebuild savoury depth using infused oils, green tops, chives, and asafoetida (hing) rather than the fructan-rich bulbs themselves.

## Wheat and rye

Wheat- and rye-based foods contribute a large share of dietary fructans: bread, pasta, couscous, crackers, breakfast cereals, and most baked goods. For the majority of people who do not have celiac disease, it is the fructan content — not the gluten protein — that drives their symptoms after eating wheat. Dose still matters: a small portion of wheat may stay under the Monash threshold even for sensitive people, so a single cracker or a small amount of pasta is often tolerated when a large bowl is not. Traditionally made sourdough, fermented slowly over many hours, and spelt sourdough both have measurably reduced fructan content, because the wild yeast and bacteria consume some of the fructans during the long proving stage.

## Cooking does not remove fructans

A persistent myth is that cooking "burns off" onion and garlic, making them safe. It does not. Fructans are heat-stable, so boiling, roasting, grilling, sautéing, or frying garlic and onion leaves the fructan content essentially intact. Worse, because fructans are water-soluble, simmering onion or garlic in a soup, curry, stock, or sauce leaches them OUT of the solid pieces and INTO the surrounding liquid — so removing the visible chunks afterwards does not make the dish low-FODMAP; the trigger has already dissolved into the broth. The one common food process that genuinely lowers fructans is long fermentation, as in authentic sourdough bread, where the fructans are metabolised before baking rather than merely heated.

## Dose thresholds and stacking

FODMAP effects are dose-dependent rather than all-or-nothing, which is central to using this information well. Monash publishes a per-serve threshold for each food, and staying within a "green" serving usually avoids symptoms, while a larger "red" serving crosses into likely trigger territory. Critically, fructans accumulate across a whole meal: a small amount of wheat bread, plus a little onion in the filling, plus a trace of garlic in the dressing can together push the total over the threshold even when each individual source, eaten alone, would have been safe. This is why portion size and the combination of foods both matter, and why an apparently "clean" meal can still provoke symptoms through stacking.
