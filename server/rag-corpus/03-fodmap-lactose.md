# Lactose — Dose Tolerance and the Dairy Ladder

Lactose is the disaccharide sugar in milk, and digesting it requires the enzyme lactase, produced in the small-intestinal lining. In lactase non-persistence — the common adult pattern across most of the world's population — lactase activity declines after early childhood, leaving much of any lactose eaten undigested. That undigested lactose then reaches the colon, where it osmotically draws water into the bowel and is fermented by bacteria into gas, producing the classic cluster of bloating, cramps, wind, and diarrhoea (NIDDK). Because lactose is also the disaccharide, the "D" in FODMAP, lactose intolerance overlaps heavily with IBS, and many people with IBS report dairy sensitivity even when formal lactase testing is normal. Understanding lactose as a dose-dependent sugar, not an all-or-nothing allergy, is the key to managing it well.

## Lactose tolerance is dose-dependent

The single most useful fact about lactose intolerance is that it is dose-dependent, not all-or-nothing. The systematic reviews commissioned for the 2010 NIH Consensus Development Conference concluded that most lactose-intolerant adults can tolerate roughly 12 grams of lactose in a single sitting — about the amount in one cup (240 mL) of milk — with no or only minor symptoms, and can handle still more when it is spread across the day and eaten together with other foods rather than on an empty stomach (Suchy et al., NIH Consensus, Ann Intern Med 2010). This is why a splash of milk in tea or a small amount of dairy used in cooking is usually harmless even for people who react badly to a large glass of milk, and it reframes management around portion and timing rather than blanket avoidance.

## High-lactose foods

The top of the "dairy ladder" holds the foods with the most lactose per serving. Regular cow's milk carries about 12-13 grams of lactose per cup, and ice cream, milkshakes, and sweetened dairy drinks are comparable or higher because they are milk-based and eaten in volume. Evaporated and condensed milk are concentrated and therefore very high. Soft, fresh cheeses such as ricotta and cottage cheese, and cream-based sauces and custards, also sit high on the ladder. Large servings of any of these concentrate the lactose dose and are the most likely to provoke symptoms, so they are the first foods to reduce in portion or swap for lower-lactose alternatives when symptoms appear.

## Moderate: yogurt and cultured dairy

Yogurt occupies a friendlier middle rung and is frequently tolerated better than an equivalent amount of plain milk. The reason is that the live bacterial cultures used to make yogurt pre-digest some of the lactose during fermentation, and those bacteria continue to supply lactase activity in the gut after eating. Kefir, a fermented milk drink with an even broader range of cultures, behaves similarly. As a result, many people who cannot manage a glass of milk can comfortably eat a normal serving of plain, unsweetened yogurt — especially varieties labelled with live or active cultures — making cultured dairy a practical way to keep dairy nutrition in the diet without the full lactose hit.

## Low or minimal: aged cheese, butter, lactose-free

The bottom of the ladder is well tolerated by the great majority of lactose-intolerant people. Hard, aged cheeses — cheddar, parmesan, Swiss, gouda, and similar — contain very little lactose, because most of it drains off with the whey during cheesemaking and the small remainder is broken down as the cheese matures over weeks to years. Butter is almost pure milk fat with only trace lactose. Lactose-free milk is genuine dairy that has been treated with lactase so the lactose is already split into its component sugars; it tastes slightly sweeter but delivers zero lactose and full dairy nutrition. These options let people keep cheese, butter, and milk-style drinks in their diet essentially without symptoms.

## Practical strategies

Tolerance improves markedly with a few deliberate techniques rather than strict avoidance. Spread dairy across the day instead of concentrating it in one large serving, so the gut never faces more than roughly its 12-gram threshold at once. Pair dairy with a meal, because other food slows gastric emptying and dilutes the lactose reaching the colon. Choose the lower rungs of the ladder — hard cheese, butter, lactose-free milk, and cultured yogurt — as everyday defaults. And for occasions when a high-lactose food is unavoidable, a lactase enzyme supplement taken with the first bite supplies the missing enzyme and can prevent symptoms. Together these steps keep dairy in the diet while controlling the load.
