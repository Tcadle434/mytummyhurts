# GERD — LES Relaxants: Chocolate, Mint, Caffeine, Alcohol, Carbonation

A recognisable cluster of foods and drinks are limited in GERD because they either lower lower-oesophageal-sphincter (LES) pressure or distend the stomach with gas, both of which let stomach contents reflux more easily. The mechanisms for these items are biologically plausible and very widely cited in patient guidance. It is important to be candid, though, that the controlled clinical evidence for eliminating any single one of them is generally weak, and the effect varies considerably from person to person (Kaltenbach et al., Arch Intern Med 2006). The right way to use this list is as a set of personalised triggers to test and confirm individually, not as a blanket prohibition that every person with reflux must adopt wholesale.

## Chocolate

Chocolate is one of the more commonly reported dietary reflux triggers, and it has a specific proposed mechanism. It contains methylxanthines — theobromine and a smaller amount of caffeine — which relax the smooth muscle of the lower oesophageal sphincter and can therefore allow reflux. Cocoa also stimulates gastric acid secretion, adding a second route to symptoms. The effect tends to be dose-related and compounded by the company chocolate keeps: rich chocolate desserts, ganache, and cream-laden confections pair the methylxanthine effect with a substantial fat and sugar load, and the fat independently slows gastric emptying, so a heavy chocolate dessert late in the evening is a particularly reliable provocation for susceptible people.

## Peppermint and spearmint

Peppermint and spearmint relax smooth muscle throughout the gut, including the lower oesophageal sphincter, which is why the traditional after-dinner mint can paradoxically worsen heartburn rather than settle the stomach as intended. Mint teas and mint-flavoured confections can have the same effect in sensitive individuals. There is an important distinction to draw here, however: enteric-coated peppermint oil capsules, which are formulated to bypass the stomach and release lower down in the intestine, are used therapeutically to relax intestinal spasm in IBS and represent a completely different exposure from mint eaten or sipped where it can act on the oesophageal sphincter. The relevant caution for reflux is dietary and beverage mint, not the enteric-coated IBS preparation.

## Coffee, caffeine, and decaf

Coffee and caffeine can both stimulate gastric acid secretion and, at least in some studies, lower LES pressure, making coffee a frequent suspect in reflux. The most useful and often-overlooked point is that decaffeinated coffee still stimulates acid: compounds in the coffee bean beyond caffeine itself contribute to acid secretion, so switching to decaf reduces but does not eliminate the provocation for people whose symptoms track with coffee. Strong tea, energy drinks, and other caffeinated beverages carry the caffeine component of the effect. As with the rest of this cluster, individual variation is large — some people with GERD tolerate coffee without any trouble — so a personal trial of reducing or removing it is more informative than assuming it must be cut.

## Alcohol and the beer triple-hit

Alcohol contributes to reflux by relaxing the lower oesophageal sphincter and by impairing the oesophagus's ability to clear acid that has refluxed, so it both lets acid up and slows its removal. Beer deserves particular mention as a compound trigger, because it delivers three reflux-promoting factors simultaneously: the alcohol itself, carbonation that distends the stomach, and a fermentable-carbohydrate load that can add bloating. This stacking is why beer often provokes both heartburn and abdominal distension at once. Wine and spirits carry the alcohol and clearance effects without the carbonation, and higher intakes and drinking close to bedtime both worsen the impact, so timing and quantity matter alongside the choice of drink.

## Carbonation

Carbonated drinks — sodas, colas, sparkling water, seltzer, and tonic — promote reflux mainly through a mechanical route: the dissolved carbon dioxide is released as gas in the stomach, distending it, driving belching, and triggering the transient lower-oesophageal-sphincter relaxations that carry acid upward during a belch. Because this distension effect comes from the gas rather than from sugar or acidity, it applies just as much to plain and sugar-free sparkling waters as to sweetened sodas, which surprises people who switch to sparkling water expecting relief. For someone whose reflux flares after fizzy drinks, flat water and non-carbonated beverages are the straightforward substitution to test.

## Test your own triggers

Because the controlled evidence for each item in this cluster is limited and individual responses vary so much, current guidance favours identifying and removing a person's OWN reliably reproducible triggers rather than eliminating every food on the list at the same time. Cutting all of chocolate, mint, coffee, alcohol, and carbonation at once is restrictive, hard to sustain, and often unnecessary, since most people react strongly to only one or two of them. A short, deliberate trial — remove a single suspected item for a week or two, then reintroduce it and watch the response — is the most practical way to learn which of these actually matters for a given individual and which can safely stay in the diet.
