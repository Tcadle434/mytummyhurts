# GERD — Dietary Fat, Meal Size, and Timing

Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GERD) occurs when stomach contents flow back up into the oesophagus, most often through transient relaxations of the lower oesophageal sphincter (TLESRs) or through chronically low resting pressure in that sphincter. Diet and meal patterns influence reflux through two main levers: how often the sphincter relaxes, and how much acidic content is sitting in the stomach available to reflux (NIDDK). Of all the dietary factors, the amount of fat in a meal, the size of the meal, and its timing relative to lying down are the most mechanistically important — and, usefully, the most within a person's control. This document covers those structural factors; specific trigger foods that relax the sphincter chemically are covered separately.

## Dietary fat and fried foods

High-fat and deep-fried meals are traditionally limited in GERD for two mechanistic reasons: fat can delay gastric emptying, which leaves food and acid in the stomach longer and increases the window for reflux, and fat triggers release of the hormone cholecystokinin, which lowers lower-oesophageal-sphincter pressure and prolongs acid exposure. It is worth being honest that the mechanistic case here is stronger than the clinical-trial evidence — controlled studies of fat restriction have produced mixed results, and fat's role may be intertwined with the fact that fatty meals are also large ones. High-fat, fried, and very rich meals are therefore best treated as a reasonable, individualised target to test rather than a proven universal rule (Kaltenbach et al., Arch Intern Med 2006).

## Meal size and gastric distension

Large meals are one of the more consistent dietary provocations of reflux, and the mechanism is largely mechanical. A big meal distends the stomach, and gastric distension directly increases the frequency of transient lower-oesophageal-sphincter relaxations while also raising intragastric pressure — both of which push contents up toward the oesophagus. Eating smaller, more frequent meals reduces this distension and is one of the more reliably helpful changes a person can make. In practice, the same food that causes heartburn as a large single plateful may be comfortable when eaten as a smaller portion, so portion size often matters as much as, or more than, the specific ingredients on the plate.

## Late meals and lying down

Reflux is markedly worse when a person is lying down, because gravity is no longer helping to keep stomach contents down or to clear any acid that does escape back into the stomach. Eating within roughly three hours of bedtime is associated with more nocturnal reflux and disturbed sleep. The classic and predictable provocation for night-time symptoms is a late, large, fatty meal followed soon afterward by lying flat — every risk factor stacked at once. Two simple structural fixes address this directly: leaving a longer gap between the last meal and bedtime, and raising the head of the bed so that the upper body is elevated through the night, which uses gravity to reduce acid contact time in the oesophagus.

## Weight has the strongest evidence

Among all the lifestyle and dietary factors studied in GERD, two stand out for having the strongest supporting evidence: weight loss in people who are overweight, and raising the head of the bed for those with night-time reflux (Ness-Jensen et al., Clin Gastroenterol Hepatol 2016). Excess abdominal weight increases intragastric pressure and mechanically promotes reflux, so for many people weight management does more to control symptoms than eliminating any individual trigger food. This is an important corrective to the common assumption that GERD is mainly about avoiding certain foods: the highest-evidence interventions are structural — body weight, meal size, and posture — rather than a long list of forbidden ingredients.

## Practical priorities

For most people the higher-yield changes are structural rather than a sprawling blocklist of foods. The core measures are to eat smaller portions, finish eating earlier in the evening and avoid lying down soon after, stay upright and let the meal settle, raise the head of the bed for nocturnal symptoms, and — where relevant — lose excess weight. Cutting the fattiest and largest meals tackles the underlying mechanism of delayed emptying and gastric distension directly. Trigger-food avoidance still has a place for individuals with clear, reproducible reactions, but it is generally a secondary layer on top of these structural changes rather than the foundation of GERD management.
