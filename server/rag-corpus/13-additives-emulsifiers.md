# Dietary Emulsifiers and Additives (CMC, Polysorbate-80)

Emulsifiers are detergent-like additives that keep oil and water blended and improve the texture, mouthfeel, and shelf life of processed foods, and they are genuinely ubiquitous across the modern ultra-processed food supply. Two in particular — carboxymethylcellulose (CMC, also sold as cellulose gum, additive number E466) and polysorbate-80 (P80, E433) — have drawn scientific attention for their possible effects on the gut. It is essential to frame this topic accurately from the outset: the evidence is early, it comes mostly from animal work with only preliminary human data, and it does not yet support firm dietary rules or a claim that these additives cause disease at the levels people normally eat. This document lays out what the studies actually found and, just as importantly, what they do not yet establish.

## The animal evidence

The initial and most influential signal came from mouse studies. Chassaing and colleagues, publishing in Nature in 2015, added low, dietary-relevant concentrations of either CMC or polysorbate-80 to the drinking water of mice and observed a consistent set of changes: the composition of the gut microbiota shifted, the protective inner mucus layer that normally separates gut bacteria from the intestinal wall became thinner, bacteria were able to encroach closer to the epithelium, and the animals developed low-grade intestinal inflammation along with features of metabolic syndrome. In mouse strains genetically predisposed to colitis, the emulsifiers worsened that colitis. The findings were striking and mechanistically coherent, but they were obtained in mice at controlled exposures, which limits how directly they can be applied to human diets.

## The first human trial

The key step toward human relevance was a randomised controlled feeding trial in healthy adults, known as the FRESH study and published by Chassaing and colleagues in Gastroenterology in 2022. In it, consumption of CMC altered the composition of the gut microbiota and the faecal metabolome and reduced microbial diversity compared with an additive-free control diet, and a subset of participants showed bacteria encroaching into the normally near-sterile inner mucus layer — the same phenomenon flagged in the mouse work. This was the first controlled human evidence that a common emulsifier can perturb the gut environment. Its limitations must be held alongside the finding, however: the study was short in duration and small in size, effects varied between individuals, and it measured biological changes rather than a defined disease outcome.

## The proposed mechanism

The hypothesised pathway tying these observations together is mucus-layer thinning leading to closer contact between gut bacteria and the intestinal epithelium, which in turn could drive low-grade inflammation and altered bacterial fermentation. In principle, a thinner mucus barrier and a shifted, less diverse microbiota might contribute to bloating, altered bowel habits, and IBS-type symptoms in susceptible people. It is important to be precise about the status of this idea: the mechanism is biologically plausible and supported by the microbiome and mucus findings, but the leap from "measurable changes in gut biology" to "causes clinical symptoms or disease in ordinary eaters" remains a hypothesis under investigation rather than an established clinical fact.

## Where these additives appear

CMC, polysorbate-80, and related emulsifiers and gums are common in a predictable set of products: ice cream and frozen desserts, many sauces and salad dressings, plant-based milks and other dairy alternatives, baked goods and breads with long shelf lives, protein and snack bars, and various processed dairy products. Reading ingredient lists for terms such as "cellulose gum," "carboxymethylcellulose," "polysorbate," "mono- and diglycerides," and similar gums quickly reveals how widespread these additives are, and how concentrated they can be in heavily processed foods compared with home cooking. This visibility is useful for anyone who wants to reduce their exposure while the science matures.

## An honest evidence caveat

The responsible bottom line is that this is an active and evolving area of research, not settled nutritional science, and it should be presented that way. The human data remain limited and short-term, individual responses appear to differ, and no safe or unsafe intake threshold has been established for these additives at dietary levels. It is therefore reasonable — but explicitly not proven — to note a high ultra-processed emulsifier load as one possible contributor to gut symptoms in some people. The defensible, evidence-aligned takeaway is simply that a diet built around whole and minimally processed foods reduces exposure to these additives as a byproduct, rather than any claim that a specific emulsifier has been confirmed to cause harm to humans at the amounts found in ordinary food.
