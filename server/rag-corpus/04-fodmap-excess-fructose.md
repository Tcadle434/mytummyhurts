# Excess Fructose — Fruit, Honey, and Syrups

"Excess fructose" is the monosaccharide FODMAP, the "M," and it refers specifically to free fructose present beyond what the gut can readily absorb. Fructose is taken up in the small intestine partly by the low-capacity GLUT5 transporter, which is easily saturated. Crucially, the presence of glucose switches on a second, higher-capacity absorption route, so fructose is absorbed well when accompanied by an equal or greater amount of glucose. A food only becomes a FODMAP problem when it contains more fructose than glucose: the surplus free fructose is then malabsorbed, draws water into the bowel osmotically, and is fermented in the colon. This is why the fructose-to-glucose BALANCE of a food matters far more than its total fructose content, and why "sugary" is not the same as "high excess fructose" (Monash University FODMAP).

## High-fructose fruits

Apples, pears, mangoes, watermelon, and cherries all contain fructose in clear excess of glucose, which makes them common triggers, particularly in larger servings. Figs and several dried fruits are also fructose-dominant. The important and reassuring counterpoint is that many fruits are perfectly safe: bananas, oranges and other citrus, grapes, kiwi, strawberries, blueberries, and most other berries are either glucose-balanced or glucose-dominant and are usually well tolerated in normal portions. This distinction matters because people with IBS often wrongly conclude that "all fruit" upsets them and cut it out entirely. In reality it is a specific set of fructose-dominant fruits that cause trouble, and a varied fruit intake built around the balanced ones is both tolerable and nutritionally valuable.

## Honey and syrups

Honey and agave nectar are strongly fructose-dominant sweeteners and are among the most frequent hidden triggers, slipping into tea, breakfast bowls, salad dressings, marinades, and "natural" or "refined-sugar-free" sweets where they are perceived as healthy. High-fructose corn syrup (HFCS), used heavily in sodas, sweetened drinks, sauces, and processed foods, adds a large, concentrated free-fructose load in liquid form that is absorbed quickly and in quantity. Because all of these are pure fructose sources without matching glucose, they behave very differently from ordinary table sugar and can provoke symptoms even in small amounts. Scanning ingredient lists for honey, agave, and corn syrup is a practical habit for anyone tracking fructose.

## Fruit juice and dried fruit

Two everyday forms of fruit dramatically concentrate fructose and are easy to overlook. Juicing strips out the fibre and concentrates the sugars, so a single glass of apple or pear juice can hold the fructose of several whole fruits consumed in one go, delivered rapidly without the bulk that would normally limit intake. Dried fruit concentrates fructose even further into small, moreish portions — a handful of raisins, sultanas, dates, or dried apple carries the sugar of a much larger amount of fresh fruit. Both juice and dried fruit therefore deliver a fructose dose well above what the same fruit would give when eaten whole and fresh, and both are common culprits behind symptoms that seem to come "out of nowhere."

## Glucose co-ingestion helps

Because glucose drives the co-absorption of fructose across the gut wall, deliberately adding glucose or choosing glucose-balanced foods can reduce or prevent symptoms. This mechanism explains a useful practical point: ordinary table sugar, sucrose, is a 50/50 pairing of glucose and fructose and is therefore often tolerated far better than an equivalent sweetness from honey, which is fructose-heavy. It also explains why a fructose-dominant fruit may be more comfortably eaten alongside a glucose-containing food than on its own. Rather than eliminating all sweetness, people managing fructose malabsorption can often shift toward glucose-balanced sources and pair fruit sensibly to stay within tolerance.

## Dose and individual variation

Fructose malabsorption is strongly dose-dependent and varies widely between individuals — a substantial share of the general population malabsorbs fructose to some degree without ever developing symptoms, because whether malabsorption becomes discomfort depends on gut sensitivity and the total load. The practical implications are that small portions of high-fructose fruits are frequently tolerated even when large ones are not, that spacing fruit across the day beats eating several pieces at once, and that choosing glucose-balanced fruits as everyday staples keeps intake comfortable. As with all FODMAPs, the aim is to find a personal threshold and eat to it, not to banish fruit or sweetness from the diet.
